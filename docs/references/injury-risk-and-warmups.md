## Research and population matching

Before prescribing, record the closest available match for:

- age and training age
- sex when the evidence shows a meaningful difference
- maturation/growth context
- recreational, scholastic, club, collegiate, or elite level
- outdoor or indoor format
- field player or goalkeeper
- practice, match, tournament, preseason, or return after a layoff
- surface, footwear, climate, and available space

Extract from injury studies: exposure definition, injury definition, incidence, burden/time loss, body region, diagnosis category, acute/overuse, contact/noncontact, mechanism, practice/competition split, recurrence, confidence intervals, and study limitations. Never apply an elite, adult, collegiate, or male rate to youth girls without naming the mismatch.

Prefer systematic reviews, prospective surveillance, controlled intervention studies, consensus statements, and governing-body surveillance. Use general coaching articles only for exercise variations, not to establish injury incidence or prevention efficacy.

## Injury-mechanism classification

Classify the problem before choosing exercises:

- **Noncontact lower extremity:** strength, neuromuscular control, landing, braking, cutting, balance, and progressive exposure may be relevant.
- **Muscle/tendon or workload related:** capacity, progressive loading, dynamic preparation, recovery, and schedule management may be relevant.
- **Ball/stick or collision trauma:** technique, protective equipment, rules, practice design, and exposure management dominate; do not imply stretching prevents these injuries.
- **Concussion/head injury:** recognition, removal, medical management, rules, technique, and equipment; never market a physical warm-up as concussion prevention.
- **Goalkeeper impact/exposure:** goalkeeper technique, equipment, landing/diving preparation, mobility, and exposure management require a position-specific analysis.

Distinguish injury incidence from injury burden. A frequent minor injury and a rarer severe injury may deserve different priorities.

## Cross-sport transfer test

Use another sport only after checking:

1. Population match: age, sex, maturation, and playing level.
2. Movement match: sprint, cut, brake, land, rotate, maintain low posture, or repeat efforts.
3. Mechanism match: the same tissue and injury mechanism, not merely the same body part.
4. Environment match: surface, footwear, space, contact, and equipment.
5. Exposure match: duration, substitutions, work:rest pattern, and weekly load.
6. Intervention fit: the exercise and studied dose can be delivered safely in field hockey.

Useful but conditional comparisons include:

| Field-hockey question | Potential cross-applicable evidence |
|---|---|
| Female-youth knee and ankle risk reduction | Soccer, basketball, lacrosse, team handball, volleyball |
| Acceleration and hamstring preparation | Soccer, rugby, sprint training |
| Braking and change of direction | Soccer, basketball, lacrosse, team handball |
| Groin/adductor capacity | Soccer and ice hockey, with surface/mechanics caveats |
| Repeated sprint preparation | Soccer, lacrosse, rugby sevens, team handball |
| Landing control | Basketball, volleyball, gymnastics, goalkeeper research |
| Low posture/trunk endurance | Field hockey first; lacrosse and ice hockey selectively |

Label the resulting recommendation: **direct**, **near-direct**, **cross-sport**, **general consensus**, or **practice-based**.

## Prescription standard

For each recommended movement or exercise, provide:

- target demand or modifiable mechanism
- exercise and one essential technique cue
- sets/repetitions, time, or distance
- intensity or speed progression
- rest or flow into the next movement
- weekly/session frequency when relevant
- progression and regression rule
- evidence label
- limits of the claim

Do not turn association into causation. Do not claim an exercise prevents an injury merely because that injury is common. Do not prescribe an adult or elite dose to youth by default.

## Warm-up construction

A warm-up is preparation, not conditioning. Build it in this order:

1. **Raise:** continuous easy locomotion that increases temperature.
2. **Mobilize dynamically:** move the ranges demanded by the session, emphasizing ankles/calves, hamstrings, hips/groin, trunk, and shoulders as applicable.
3. **Activate and coordinate:** rehearse low posture, single-leg control, lateral/crossover movement, balance, and trunk control.
4. **Potentiate:** progressively accelerate, brake, cut, react, and perform stick-and-ball actions near session speed.

Match the time budget. Youth consensus guidance commonly supports a 5-10 minute dynamic warm-up, but an injury-reduction program studied as a longer structured intervention must not be compressed and represented as equivalent without evidence.

Avoid long static holds immediately before explosive work unless an athlete-specific professional has directed them. Finish warm and sharp, not breathless. In cold weather or after a long stoppage, increase the raise/progression time.

## Common dynamic-warm-up exercise bank

This is a selection bank, not a mandatory circuit. Choose movements based on the demand analysis and give a dose.

| Movement | Primary use | Key cue |
|---|---|---|
| Catch the chicken / hamstring scoop | Dynamic hamstrings and hip hinge | Heel forward, toe up, hips back, sweep hands low |
| Frankenstein / toy soldier | Dynamic hamstrings and reciprocal coordination | Controlled straight-leg lift; stay tall |
| Walking quad pull | Quadriceps and hip flexor movement | Knees close; do not arch the back |
| Open/close the gate | Hip rotation | Move through the hip without twisting the planted knee |
| Walking lunge with reach/rotation | Hips, legs, trunk | Front knee tracks over foot; rotate through upper back |
| Lateral lunge | Adductors and lateral hips | Sit into one hip; keep the other leg long |
| Carioca / grapevine | Crossover coordination and hip/trunk rotation | Begin controlled; rotate hips without tangling feet |
| Lateral shuffle | Lateral coordination and hockey posture | Hips low; do not click feet together |
| High-knee march/run | Sprint posture and hip action | Tall trunk; step down under the body |
| Butt kick | Dynamic knee flexion | Quick relaxed rhythm; avoid excessive back arch |
| A-skip / skipping | Rhythm, stiffness, sprint coordination | Step down beneath the hips |
| Ankle rocks / calf raises | Ankle range and calf preparation | Knee tracks over toes; control the heel |
| Low pogo hops | Ankle stiffness and elastic preparation | Quiet, small contacts; stop if painful |
| Inchworm | Posterior chain, shoulders, trunk | Move with control; avoid sagging through the trunk |
| Arm circles | Shoulders | Progress from small to larger controlled circles |
| Arm hugs/swings | Shoulders and upper back | Alternate which arm crosses on top |
| Trunk rotation with stick | Upper-back rotation | Keep hips controlled; rotate through the torso |
| Progressive acceleration | First steps and sprint readiness | Build from submaximal to near-session speed |
| Accelerate-and-brake | Deceleration readiness | Lower hips and stop under control, not with one reaching step |
| Planned/reactive cut | Change-of-direction readiness | Plant under the body and push away cleanly |
| Pass-and-move | Stick, feet, scanning, session specificity | Move after passing; receive with active feet |

## Starter evidence base

Use these as starting points, then search for newer reviews and population-specific evidence when the request depends on current findings:

- Barboza et al. **A Warm-Up Program to Reduce Injuries in Youth Field Hockey Players: A Quasi-Experiment** (2019): https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6522090/
- Hollander et al. **Epidemiology of injuries in outdoor and indoor hockey players over one season** (2018): https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/52/17/1091
- Cornelissen et al. **A systematic review of injuries in recreational field hockey** (2020): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32698730/
- Nedimyer et al. **Epidemiology of Injuries in NCAA Women's Field Hockey, 2014-2015 Through 2018-2019** (2021): https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8293883/
- Dick et al. **Descriptive Epidemiology of Collegiate Women's Field Hockey Injuries, 1988-1989 Through 2002-2003** (2007): https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1941291/
- Gouttebarge et al. **Development of an intervention for prevention of lower-extremity injuries in recreational field hockey** (2018): https://bmjopensem.bmj.com/content/4/1/e000425
- NSCA **Youth Resistance Training: Updated Position Statement** (2009): https://www.nsca.com/globalassets/about/position-statements/position_stand_youth_resistance_training---2009.pdf
- NSCA **Position Statement on Long-Term Athletic Development** (2016): https://www.nsca.com/globalassets/about/position-statements/nsca_position_statement_long-term_athletic_development.pdf

When reporting numerical rates or intervention effects, open the source and verify the population, denominator, confidence interval, and study design. Do not rely on a search-result snippet.
